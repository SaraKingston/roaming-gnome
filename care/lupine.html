Lupine

Tall, dramatic flower spikes in purple, pink, blue, and white, rising above palmate foliage. Lupines are a showstopper in the early summer garden and a favorite of bumblebees.

☀️
Sunlight
Full sun to part shade
💧
Water
Moderate, even moisture
🌼
Type
Perennial (zones 4–8)
🪴
Soil
Slightly acidic, well-drained

Growing Basics

  • Lupine is a perennial in Minnesota (zones 4–8). It comes back each year, though it tends to be shorter-lived than some perennials (3–5 years is typical) and may need refreshing with new plants over time.
  • Bloom time: Late spring to early summer (May–June in Minnesota). Seed-grown plants typically bloom in their second year.
  • Sunlight: Full sun to light afternoon shade. In hot summers, some protection from intense afternoon sun helps blooms last longer.
  • Soil: Well-drained, slightly acidic soil (pH 6.0–7.0). Lupines are members of the legume family and fix their own nitrogen — they don't need or want rich, fertilized soil.
  • Transplanting: Plant in spring after the last frost. Handle roots with care — lupines have taproots and don't like disturbance. Plant the whole root ball and firm in gently.
  • Watering: Keep soil evenly moist, especially during establishment. Avoid waterlogged conditions — good drainage is essential.
  • Fertilization: Not needed. As a legume, lupine fixes nitrogen from the air. Fertilizing can actually make them less vigorous.

Taproot Note — Important!

  • Lupines have a deep taproot that makes them resent transplanting. Plant them in their permanent spot and avoid moving them again once established.
  • Do not pinch the growing tip — lupines need the main stem to produce their flower spike.

Perennial Notes

  • These plants were grown from seed this year. Expect foliage growth and root development in year one; blooms typically come in year two.
  • After blooming, deadhead promptly to prevent self-seeding and encourage a possible second flush of smaller spikes.
  • If leaves look mottled or powdery, that's often powdery mildew — good air circulation helps. It typically appears after blooming and doesn't harm the plant much at that point.
  • A magnet for bumblebees, who are the primary pollinators of lupine flowers.
